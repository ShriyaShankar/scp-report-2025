This report captures insights inferred from the self-reported data (quantitative and qualitative) by the students of Rajalakshmi Children Foundation, after undergoing Phase 1 of Sitara Champions Program. These are students from low-income households across districts of Karnataka. 330 students in class 9 underwent the 1st phase of Sitara Champions Program between Aug 2025 - Feb 2026. Our gratitude to EFIL Educational Services for sharing their rich curriculum and for the Pratibha Poshak team for ensuring such a strong collaboration.

Engagement & Dropout

It is important to understand the learning design of SCP. We follow a flipped classroom approach. Students first learn from videos by themselves, submit an activity applying the knowledge in their life, receive 1-1 feedback from their akka/anna for the activity and then the 20 students attend the disucssion call for reflection and cross-learning.

📉
Sharpest dropout between M1 → M3
Attendance drops from 82% to 67% across the first three modules - a 15-point fall. After M3, attendance stabilizes around 64–67%, suggesting a "committed core" forms early. Whoever is lost by M3 likely stays lost.
✍️
Submission fatigue in final modules
Activity submissions decline steadily and fall to just 52% by M8 - 30 points below M1. Even students who attend M7 and M8 are increasingly skipping the activity, which is a habit/motivation issue worth addressing structurally.
📊
~1 in 5 students low-engaged throughout
19.8% of students attended only 0–3 of 8 modules. At the other end, 23.4% attended all 8 and 39% submitted all 8 activities - a healthy "power user" group who likely drive the strongest outcomes.

Key finding: Attendance is the lever

Both inputs matter, but attendance is a stronger predictor of better outcomes in the data. Students who attended 6+ modules scored significantly higher than those who attended 3 or fewer (4.33 vs 4.14, p=0.007).

One likely reason discussion calls outperform independent activity completion is the quality of meaning-making that happens in a shared space. When students complete activities on their own, their learning is limited by their current interpretation of the concept. In contrast, discussion calls allow them to deepen understanding through input from their akka/anna and peers, enabling richer assimilation.
Additionally, many students are conditioned by the school system to look for the "right answer." In self-discovery work, where there is no single correct response. This can create confusion or superficial engagement when working alone. The 1-1 feedback loops and live discussions appear to play a critical role in helping students process the concepts more deeply and translate them into personal insight and action.

🌟
Self-knowledge tops the chart
"Understanding Myself" was chosen by 70% of respondents - by far the most resonant module. Students repeatedly said this was the first time they had ever been asked to reflect on themselves. Phrases like "I didn't know I had skills" and "now I know who I am" appeared across responses, indicating the module is filling a genuine developmental gap. Goals & Time Management resonated strongly for its practicality. Many students reported creating a timetable for the first time and linked it directly to improved exam performance.
🧠
5W1H became a real-life tool
The 5W1H framework is the single most mentioned tool in the entire survey. Students report using it during fights with siblings, friends, and parents. One standout response described a student who used SCP tools to mediate between two friends who had stopped talking — eventually reconciling them by making them co-captains of a cricket team.
🔄
Shift from blame → responsibility
Many students explicitly referenced the MC/VC framing when describing dealing with difficult circumstances. Students have reported a shift from blaming others to taking personal responsibility. One student describes stopping mid-anger and thinking about the MC/VC concept.
📅
Self-direction in the making
Students mentioned learning cooking, drawing, rangoli, Bharatanatyam, calisthenics, knitting, cycling, chess, flute, painting, singing, coding, and even Japanese/Korean via Duolingo. Several explicitly connected this shift to the Lifelong Learner module, suggesting the module is not just changing mindset but activating real-world exploratory behavior.
🌟
Goals and Vision
Academic goals dominate, likely due to proximity of exams, with many students creating SMART goals and Power Plans for FA4/SA2. Several report concrete outcomes like full marks or first rank. Personal goals are also common: exercising, waking earlier, reducing phone use, and learning new skills. These appear genuinely student-initiated rather than formulaic. Longer-term aspirations frequently include scientist, doctor, teacher, and engineer. A smaller but important subset of students explicitly connect daily actions to long-term vision, suggesting the My Life Vision module is beginning to do its intended work.

Mentor (Akka/Anna) Effect

WE WALK THE TALK

Our akkas and annas play a very crucial role in SCP. Each akka/anna is selected after a thorough screening process. The mentors undergo extensive training for 3 months. During the training, each akka/anna undergoes all modules in the same way as their student - learn from a video, do the activity, receive 1-1 feedback from the Sitara Akka team and participate in the discussion call.

The mentors and the team in Sitara Akka aren't really mentors. we are walking alongside our students, learning together, holding the mirror for our students whenever needed.

👩‍🏫
Mentor variance is real but modest - 0.38 points gap between top and bottom
The top mentor (A, 4.53) outperforms the bottom (S, 4.16) by about 0.37 points on a 5-point scale - statistically real but not dramatic. The overall SD across mentor means is just 0.10. This suggests the program structure itself creates a strong floor, with mentors able to lift outcomes by ~10% through their individual approach. Sharing practices from top-performing mentors is worth doing.
06

Areas for Improvement

Prioritized Improvement Areas - Based on Data
1

Plug the M1→M3 dropout hole

Attendance falls 15 points in the first 3 modules - from 82% to 67%. Since attendance is your strongest predictor of outcomes (r=0.21, p=0.0004), every student you retain through M3 is a meaningful impact gain. Consider: peer accountability pairs, mentor check-ins after M2, or a mid-program engagement nudge.

2

Reverse the late-program submission drop

Activity submissions fall from 83% (M1) to just 52% (M8) - a 31-point collapse. Students are showing up but not completing activities. This fatigue likely also weakens the habit formation that the program aims to build. Consider shorter, higher-quality activities in M6–M8, or gamified accountability.

3

More frequent sessions

Multiple students ask for classes every Sunday (rather than alternate Sundays), and some ask for classes on holidays too. Some students have asked to add modules on communication skills, dealing with laziness, technology/computers, and Spoken English.

4

Codify what top mentors do differently

There's a ~0.37-point gap between the best and weakest-performing mentor clusters. Run a structured knowledge-sharing session with top mentors to understand their approach, particularly how they give feedback and build trust, and embed those practices into mentor training for the next cohort.

5

More participation from all students

Several mention that some students just stay silent during sessions and want a norm of everyone speaking.

07

Summary

This is the third iteration of the Sitara Champions Program. In Version 1, we built fast and intuitively - shooting videos on our phones, editing them ourselves, and assembling modules and activities based largely on instinct. That program concluded before we had fully settled into the model.
In Version 2, we focused on strengthening the mentorship layer. We refined our mentor selection and training processes and built better systems to help each akka/anna feel equipped in their role. However, we fell short on one critical front: we did not capture usable data to understand the program's true impact.
Version 3, which we have just concluded, marks a significant step forward. We improved the curriculum, strengthened the content, tightened processes, and built more robust data collection systems. We also expanded SCP from a one-year intervention to a two-year journey.

Until now, much of our confidence came from powerful anecdotes shared by students and mentors. What we lacked was clarity on whether those stories reflected the typical experience or exceptional cases. The current data is largely validating of the program's effectiveness, which is deeply encouraging. At the same time, the data has surfaced clear opportunities for improvement - from module sequencing and calendaring to the kinds of support mechanisms students and mentors need. We are carefully absorbing these signals as we design the next phase. Our focus now is to build a significantly more impactful intervention that supports a student from Class 9 through Class 12, equipping them with the inner tools to navigate life with greater awareness, ownership and alignment.
